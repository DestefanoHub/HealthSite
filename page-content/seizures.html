"A seizure is a sudden surge of electrical activity in the brain" according to the Epilepsy Foundation. It usually lasts for a short period of time. This electrical activity occurs when complex chemical changes in nerve cells causes the brain cells to stop sending messages. Too much or too little activity in the brain causes the excited brain cells to tell other brain cells to stop sending messages. This is when an electrical surge occurs and causes the seizure.

Some seizures can be disabling while others can be hardly noticeable. There are two types of seizures: generalized seizures and partial seizures. The classification of the type of seizure helps doctors diagnose if a person has epilepsy or not. Generalized seizures are also known as local seizures. They occur when electrical impulses are produced throughout the entire brain. Partial seizures are also known as focal seizures. They occur when electrical impulses are produced in a relatively small part of the brain. However, as a partial seizure continues the affected area of the brain can increase.

Seizures are usually a symptom of other disorders, such as epilepsy. However, this does not mean that they should be taken any less seriously. If someone is having a seizure get personal assistance immediately.

There are six subcategories of generalized seizures. The first is a grand mal seizure which is also referred to as a tonic-clonic seizure. The symptoms of a grand mal seizure are unconsciousness, convulsions, and muscle rigidity. After the person goes into unconsciousness the body starts jerking for 30-60 seconds. Immediately after the jerking stops the body becomes stiff for 30-60 seconds. The person then goes into a deep sleep.

The next subcategory of generalized seizures is an absence seizure. This seizure occurs more often with children. The symptom of a absence seizure is brief loss of consciousness. The seizure interrupts daily activity and causes the person to stare blankly into space. This can happen multiple times throughout the day. The person is most likely unaware that they are even having a seizure since they end as abruptly as they begin. The person may feel like they are losing time.

Myoclonic seizures are another subcategory of generalized seizures. The symptoms of a myoclonic seizure are isolated jerking movements on both sides of the body. This can result in the person throwing or dropping objects violently. The person may feel like they are being electrically shocked.

The last three subcategories of generalized seizures are clonic, tonic and atonic. When a clonic seizure occurs the body begins jerking repetitively. During a tonic seizure the muscles become stiff and rigid. An atonic seizure makes a person lose muscle tone which can result in them falling.

The three subcategories for partial seizures are are simple, complex and partial seizures with secondary generalization.

The symptoms of a simple seizure are upset stomach, racing heart, diarrhea, and loss of bladder control. A simple seizure occurs when the person’s awareness is retained. There are three types of simple seizures. The first is a simple motor seizure. When this seizure occurs a person’s body will begin jerking with spasms as the muscles goes rigid. The head also turns vigorously. When a simple sensory seizure occurs, unusual sensations affect a person’s senses. Vision, hearing, smell, taste, and touch will not function properly. Simple psychological seizures are the last type of simple seizure. This type of seizure causes a person to have memory or emotional disturbances, as well as deja-vu feelings.

The symptoms of a complex seizures are automatisms. These are involuntary, repetitive, and coordinated movements such as lip smacking, chewing, fidgeting, and walking. A complex seizure causes the impairment of a person’s awareness. The person may seem to stare into space and feel or look bewildered.

A partial seizure with secondary generalization occurs when the symptoms that are initially associated with a preservation of consciousness then evolves into a loss of consciousness and convulsions.

When someone begins having a seizure it is important to remain calm. There are two treatment procedures to follow when a person begins to have a seizure: while the seizure is occurring and after the seizure.

During a seizure you need to protect the person from other injuries, by keeping them from falling or guiding them to the floor. Move any furniture or objects that might harm the person. Place the person on their side so that any fluid that might form can leak out of their mouth. Don’t apply TOO much pressure on the body and never try to hold the person down. Please keep your fingers away from the person’s mouth for they might bite you. Lastly pay attention to what the person is doing and report it to a doctor later.

    You need to pay attention to:
  1. How the person’s body moved.
  2. How long the seizure lasted.
  3. How the person acted before the seizure.
  4. How the person acted immediately after the seizure.
  5. Whether the person suffered any injuries from the seizure.

After a seizure occurs you need to check the person for injuries. If the person is not on their side turn them on it. Check to see if the person is having trouble breathing. If they are, clear the airway with your finger. Loosen the person’s clothes around the neck and waist and provide safe area for the person to rest. Do not let the person eat or drink until they are fully alert. Please stay with the person until the surroundings are familiar to the person. The last step is to take the person to the doctor.
