A person will most likely have an Allergic Reaction at some point in their life and are commonly referred as having Allergies. Allergic reactions ranges from mild cases such as water eyes or a runny nose to more life-threatening cases, in which your throat closes up. These responses to are an overreaction of the body’s immune system.

The immune system is your body’s self defense system to protect the body from harmful bacterias and viruses by producing antibodies. An Allergic Reaction occurs when the body’s immune system considers a typically non-threatening substance a threat and takes defensive actions against it. These substances are known as Allergens. Allergens can be anything; ranging from dust particles to cosmetics.

Some of the Causes of an allergic reaction are pet dander, bee stings or insects bites. Many medications like penicillin and aspirin are very common allergens and can be very unpredictable. Other allergens can be pollen, and molds. Being stung by an insect can also bring about an allergic reaction. A "Bug Bite" is actually your body reacting to the insects toxin of venom. Some people are allergic to latex, or rubber produces. The most common allergens are food. Food allergies can be nuts or shellfish, which can be quite life threatening.

Allergic Reactions are usually broken down into three cases; Mild Cases, Severe Cases, and Life-Threatening Cases. Mild Cases are the most common allergic reactions and are the easiest to handle. Severe Cases usually need medical prescriptions and can cause the need for medical assistance. Life-Threatening Cases as the name suggests are the most dangerous cases and need to be handled immediately with the proper care.

The Symptoms of Mild Cases are usually itching, nasal congestion known as rhinitis, rashes, scratchy throat, watery or itchy eyes and hives. Hives are itchy red spots on the skin. DO NOT SCRATCH THE Hives.

Treatment for Mild Allergic Reactions are taking Benadryl or antihistamine. If your symptoms worsen or do not subside after a few days see your local doctor.

Severe Cases have a range of different symptoms. The Symptoms of a Severe Cases are abdominal cramping or pain, tightness or pain in the chest, diarrhea, difficulty swallowing, dizziness or vertigo, fear or anxiety, nausea or vomiting, and flushing of the face. More serious symptoms are heart palpitations, swelling of the face, eyes, or tongue, feeling weak, wheezing, difficulty breathing, and unconsciousness. Severe and sudden allergic reactions can develop within seconds after exposure to an allergen. This is known as anaphylaxis.

To Treat Severe Allergic Reactions if the person experiencing the allergic reaction is showing some of the more serious symptoms such as swelling of the face, eyes, or tongue, call 911 or get the person to emergency facility. Also ask the person if they have an EpiPen and help them administer it if they can not administer it themselves.

If the person is showing some of the less serious symptoms of a Severe Allergic Reaction such as dizziness or diarrhea, ask the person if they are allergic to anything and remove the substance. Wait to see if the symptoms persist. If they do, take the person to your local doctor.

The Life-Threatening Cases are the most dangerous allergic reactions and should not be taken lightly. The Symptoms for a Life-Threatening Case are swelling of the airway, inability to breathe, sudden and severe drop in blood pressure.

IF THESE SYMPTOMS OCCUR PLEASE CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY, WITHOUT TREATMENT A PERSON CAN DIE WITHIN 15 MINUTES.

The first step to Treat a Life-Threatening Case is to call 911 or get personal medical assistance. Check to see if the person is breathing and provide CPR if necessary. If the person has an EpiPen help them administer it.

If the person is unconscious, lay the person flat on their back and elevate their legs. Lastly place a blanket over them. This will prevent the person from going into shock.
